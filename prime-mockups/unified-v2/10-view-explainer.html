§ 03 · GRADIENT DESCENT

The Learning Rate Problem

A neural network learns by adjusting its weights to minimize a loss function — a measure of how wrong its predictions are. The algorithm that drives this process is called gradient descent: the network repeatedly nudges its parameters in the direction that reduces the loss.

Think of it as rolling a ball down a hilly landscape toward the lowest valley. At each step, the ball moves in the direction of the steepest downward slope. The size of that step is controlled by a critical hyperparameter: the learning rate, often written as η (eta).

// Weight update rule
wnew  =  wold  −  η  ·  ∂L/∂w

// where ∂L/∂w is the gradient of the loss w.r.t. each weight
Learning Rate (η)
0.010
0.001 — very slow 1.0 — too high
Converging steadily. Loss decreasing each epoch.

The sweet spot sits somewhere between extremes. Too small and the network crawls toward a solution — training takes thousands of extra epochs and may stall in a local minimum. Too large and the updates overshoot the valley, bouncing erratically or even diverging to infinity.

Modern optimizers like Adam and RMSProp adapt the learning rate per-parameter during training, which removes some of the guesswork. But even adaptive methods require a sensible initial learning rate — typically 1e-3 is a reliable starting point.

Practical tip: Use a learning rate finder — ramp the rate from a very small value over a short training run and plot the loss. The ideal rate sits just before the loss starts to rise sharply.
