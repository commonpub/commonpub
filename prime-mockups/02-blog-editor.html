USB Power Delivery has revolutionized how we power our projects. Gone are the days of hunting for the right barrel jack or dealing with proprietary charging standards. With USB-C PD, a single connector can deliver anywhere from 5V to 48V at up to 240W — that's enough to power everything from a Raspberry Pi to a high-powered soldering iron.
What is USB Power Delivery?
USB Power Delivery is a fast charging protocol that enables much higher power levels than standard USB. It works through a negotiation process where your device "asks" the power source what voltages and currents it can provide, then selects the best option for its needs.
Pro tip: Not all USB-C cables support PD. For high-power applications, make sure you're using an e-marked cable rated for your power requirements.
The Negotiation Dance
When you plug in a USB-C PD device, an intricate conversation happens in milliseconds. The sink (your device) and source (the charger) exchange capabilities using a protocol called USB-PD BMC (Bi-phase Mark Coding) over the CC (Configuration Channel) pins.
// Example: Requesting 12V from a PD source #include "PD_UFP.h" PD_UFP_c PD_UFP; void setup() { PD_UFP.init(PD_POWER_OPTION_MAX_12V); if (PD_UFP.is_PPS_ready()) { PD_UFP.set_PPS(12000, 2000); // 12V, 2A } }
The beauty of USB-PD is that it turns power into data. Your device doesn't just consume power — it communicates its needs.

PD Trigger Boards: The Easy Way
If you don't want to implement the full PD stack yourself, PD trigger boards are your friend. These small modules handle all the negotiation and output your desired voltage.
Always verify the voltage with a multimeter before connecting your project! A misconfigured trigger board could send 20V to a 5V device.
